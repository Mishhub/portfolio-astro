Most "observability" spend is wasted. Teams ship a dashboard with forty panels
nobody reads, fire alerts that everyone mutes, and still spend an hour guessing
during an incident. The tooling isn't the problem — the model is. Observability
pays off only when each signal does a job you understand, the signals connect to
each other, and your alerts are tied to what users actually feel.

## Three signals, three jobs

Logs, metrics, and traces are not interchangeable. They answer different
questions, and using one to do another's job is how monitoring bills explode
while visibility stays poor.

- **Metrics** answer *"is something wrong, and how much?"* They're cheap,
  aggregatable numbers over time — request rate, error rate, p99 latency. You
  alert on metrics because they're cheap to evaluate continuously.
- **Logs** answer *"what exactly happened in this one case?"* They're the
  detailed event record you read *after* a metric told you where to look. Log
  with intent — structured, leveled, and sparing — not a `console.log` confetti
  cannon.
- **Traces** answer *"where did the time go across services?"* A trace stitches
  one request's path through every hop, so a 2-second checkout becomes visibly
  "1.8s of that was the inventory call."

```ts
// structured logs are queryable; string-concatenated ones are not
logger.info({
  event: "payment.captured",
  orderId,
  amountCents,
  latencyMs,
  correlationId,
});
```

The discipline is to reach for the right one. You don't grep terabytes of logs to
find out your error rate spiked — that's a metric. You don't emit a metric per
user — that's a log line.

## Correlation IDs are the glue

Three signals are useless if you can't pivot between them. A single request that
touches five services should carry one **correlation ID** (trace ID) end to end,
stamped onto every log line and propagated on every outbound call. That ID is
what turns "the error rate is up" into "show me the logs for the failing requests"
into "here's the trace where the database call timed out" — in three clicks
instead of three hours.

```ts
// propagate the id inward; generate one at the edge if absent
const correlationId = req.headers["x-correlation-id"] ?? randomUUID();
context.run({ correlationId }, () => handle(req));
```

<Callout type="tip" title="Generate at the edge, propagate everywhere">
  Mint the correlation ID at your outermost entry point — the gateway or first
  service — and pass it through every internal call and queue message. A
  correlation ID that exists in only one service is just a log field; one that
  spans the whole request is a debugger.
</Callout>

## SLOs decide what's worth caring about

Without a definition of "good enough," every blip looks like a fire and you alert
on all of them. A **Service Level Objective** is that definition: a target on a
user-facing metric over a window — "99.9% of checkout requests succeed under
500ms over 30 days."

The SLO gives you an **error budget**: the 0.1% you're allowed to fail. That
budget is the most useful number in the whole system. Burning it slowly is fine —
that's what it's for. Burning it fast is the only thing worth waking someone for.
And when the budget's healthy, you ship features instead of chasing a flaky panel
that doesn't affect anyone.

<Callout type="insight" title="The error budget is a decision tool, not a vanity metric">
  When the budget is intact, ship faster. When it's burning, freeze risky changes
  and stabilize. The SLO turns reliability from a vibe into a number both
  engineers and product can act on.
</Callout>

## Alert on symptoms, not causes

This is the single highest-leverage change most teams can make. Don't alert on
"CPU is at 90%." High CPU might mean nothing — maybe you're efficiently busy.
Alert on what the *user* experiences: the checkout success rate dropped, p99
latency crossed the SLO threshold, the error budget is burning at 10x.

Cause-based alerts have two failure modes, and you suffer both at once. They fire
when nothing is actually wrong (a node is busy but users are fine), training
everyone to ignore the pager. And they *miss* real problems your prebuilt
causes didn't anticipate — the outage caused by something you never thought to
threshold. Symptom-based alerts catch the user-facing problem regardless of cause,
then your traces and logs tell you *why*.

```yaml
# alert on the symptom users feel...
- alert: CheckoutErrorBudgetBurningFast
  expr: checkout_error_ratio > (14 * 0.001)  # 14x burn vs the 0.1% budget
  for: 5m
# ...not on a cause that may or may not matter
# (skip: node_cpu_seconds > 0.9)
```

## Putting it together

Good observability is a small, coherent system, not a pile of tools. Metrics tell
you *that* something's wrong and trip an alert when users are affected. The
correlation ID jumps you from that alert to the exact failing requests. Traces
show you *where* the latency or error lives, and logs tell you the *why* for that
one request. SLOs decide what's worth an alert at all, and error budgets decide
whether you're firefighting or shipping.

Cut the forty-panel dashboard. Define one SLO for your most important user
journey, alert on its error budget, make sure a correlation ID flows through every
service, and confirm you can pivot from an alert to a trace to a log in under a
minute. That's the version of observability that pays for itself the first time
something breaks.
